Maurice Hilleman

The most influential vaccinologist of all time

About Dr. Hilleman

Dr. Maurice Hilleman is considered by many to be the father of modern vaccines. Over the course of his career, he developed many of the vaccines that are routinely recommended for children today. By the end of his career, Dr. Hilleman had prevented pandemic flu, combined the measles-mumps-rubella vaccines (MMR), developed the first vaccine against a type of human cancer, and much more. It is likely that Dr. Hilleman’s work has saved more lives worldwide than any other scientist in history.

His Life

  • 1919
    Born in Miles City, Montana. His twin sister and mother died soon after his birth and he was raised by his aunt and uncle.
  • 1937
    Graduated from high school and was awarded a scholarship to Montana State College (now Montana State University).
  • 1941
    Graduated at the top of his class with a major in chemistry and microbiology. He furthered his education at the University of Chicago where he studied chlamydia.
  • 1943
    He married his hometown sweetheart, Thelma Mason.
  • 1944
    Completed his PhD and started working at a company called E.R. Squibb & Sons where he developed his first vaccine for the Japanese encephalitis (JE) virus.
  • 1949
    He became the chief of respiratory diseases at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research in Washington, D.C, where he studied the influenza virus.
  • 1957
    He predicted an influenza pandemic and created a vaccine that saved hundreds of thousands of American lives when the virus arrived in the U.S. He was awarded the Distinguished Service Medal from the American military. At the end of the year his daughter Jeryl Lynn was born and he began working for Merck & Co, where he remained for the rest of his career.
  • 1963
    His daughter Jeryl Lynn contracted mumps. He isolated the virus from her, and used it as the basis of a mumps vaccine. The Jeryl Lynn strain of the mumps vaccine is still used today. At the same year his wife, Thelma, died.
  • 1981
    He and his group invented a vaccine for hepatitis B by treating blood serum with pepsin, urea and formaldehyde. This was licensed in 1981, but withdrawn in 1986 in the United States when it was replaced by a vaccine that was produced in yeast.
  • 1984
    He retired from Merck at the age of 65, but he continued to contribute to science as a consultant and mentor to those still working to stop infectious diseases from harming or killing people around the world.
  • 1988
    President Ronald Regan awarded him the National Medal of Science, the highest science award given in the U.S.
  • 2005
    Died in Philadelphia on April 11, at the age of 85. At the time of his death, he was Adjunct Professor of Pediatrics at the University of Pennsylvania in Philadelphia.

Legacy

Colleagues note that Dr. Hilleman was always more concerned with preventing disease than being recognized for his efforts. Many people remain unfamiliar with Dr. Hilleman’s name, despite the fact that his work has directly impacted their lives. Dr. Hilleman’s work is estimated to save about eight million lives every year.
